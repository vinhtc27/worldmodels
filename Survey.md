# World models in AI: from Craik's mental models to billion-dollar bets

The concept of an AI system that builds and uses an internal model of its environment — a "world model" — has become one of the most consequential ideas in modern artificial intelligence. **World models now attract billions in investment, anchor the research agendas of every major AI lab, and are increasingly positioned as the missing ingredient for general intelligence.** Yet the term itself spans a remarkably wide spectrum: from lightweight latent dynamics models that help reinforcement learning agents play Atari, to massive video generators framed as "world simulators," to physics-informed neural networks that encode conservation laws in their architecture. This review traces the full arc from Kenneth Craik's 1943 insight that minds build "small-scale models of reality" through the current frontier, where Yann LeCun has left Meta to bet over a billion dollars that world models — not large language models — are the path to advanced machine intelligence.

The field has converged on a shared conviction: prediction is the foundation of intelligence. But deep disagreements persist about what should be predicted (pixels vs. abstract representations), how physics should be incorporated (learned from data vs. encoded in architecture), and whether current generative models truly understand the world or merely produce statistically plausible hallucinations. Understanding these debates requires surveying the entire landscape — cognitive science origins, model-based RL, generative foundation models, physics-informed approaches, robotics applications, and the theoretical arguments that animate them.

## Eighty years of building internal models of the world

The intellectual lineage of world models begins with Kenneth Craik, a Scottish psychologist at Cambridge, who proposed in his 1943 book *The Nature of Explanation* that the mind constructs "small-scale models" of reality to anticipate events, reason, and plan. Craik articulated three processes: translation of external phenomena into internal representations, derivation of new representations through inference, and retranslation back into predictions about the external world. He drew explicit analogies to mechanical devices — anti-aircraft predictors, calculating machines, Kelvin's tidal predictor — arguing that brains and machines share fundamental principles of predictive modeling. Craik died in a bicycle accident at age 31 in 1945, but his insight became foundational.

Philip Johnson-Laird formalized Craik's intuition into a full cognitive theory in his 1983 *Mental Models*, demonstrating that human reasoning operates by constructing and manipulating mental models of situations rather than applying formal logic. The parallel development of **predictive coding** in neuroscience — the idea that the brain actively predicts sensory input and processes only prediction errors — provided a complementary computational framework, culminating in Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle, which cast all brain function as world-model-based Bayesian inference.

The AI-specific lineage begins with Jürgen Schmidhuber's remarkably prescient 1990 technical report at TU Munich, which introduced a system combining two recurrent neural networks: a **controller** that selects actions and a **world model** that predicts their consequences. The controller could plan ahead by "dreaming" — rolling out imagined trajectories through the world model without interacting with the real environment. The same paper introduced artificial curiosity through adversarial networks, where prediction error serves as intrinsic motivation. Rich Sutton's contemporaneous Dyna architecture (1990–1991) independently established the integrate-learn-plan paradigm for model-based reinforcement learning. Schmidhuber continued developing these ideas through the 1990s and 2000s, publishing "On Learning to Think" in 2015, which described RNN-based agents that learn to query their world models for abstract reasoning.

The concept crystallized for the modern deep learning era with Ha and Schmidhuber's 2018 NeurIPS paper "World Models," which provided an elegant and accessible architecture: a **Variational Autoencoder** (V) compresses each image frame into a latent vector, an **MDN-RNN** (M) predicts the probability distribution of future latent states, and a deliberately simple **linear controller** (C) maps latent states to actions. With fewer than 1,000 parameters in the controller, the system solved CarRacing and VizDoom, and agents could be trained entirely inside their own hallucinated dreams. The accompanying interactive web article became iconic and inspired the subsequent explosion of world model research.

## The reinforcement learning lineage: from PlaNet to DreamerV4

The model-based RL world model family represents the most direct computational realization of Craik's vision: learn a dynamics model, imagine future trajectories, and optimize behavior through mental simulation.

**PlaNet** (Hafner et al., ICML 2019) introduced the **Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM)**, which became the architectural backbone for the entire Dreamer lineage. The RSSM splits latent state into a deterministic component (GRU hidden state for memory) and a stochastic component (sampled latent variable for uncertainty). PlaNet demonstrated that learned latent dynamics could solve continuous control tasks from pixels with **50× fewer episodes** than model-free methods, using Cross-Entropy Method planning in latent space.

**Dreamer v1** (Hafner et al., ICLR 2020) replaced PlaNet's expensive online planning with actor-critic learning in imagination. An actor and critic network train entirely on imagined trajectories rolled out in the world model's latent space — no images need to be generated during behavior learning. This "imagine then act" paradigm became the standard approach. **DreamerV2** (ICLR 2021) introduced categorical discrete latent representations (32 variables × 32 classes) and KL balancing, becoming the **first world model agent to achieve human-level performance on the full Atari benchmark** of 55 games.

**DreamerV3** (Hafner et al., 2023; published in *Nature* April 2025) achieved a breakthrough in generality: a single algorithm with **fixed hyperparameters** mastering over 150 tasks across radically different domains. Key innovations include symlog predictions for handling different observation scales, percentile return normalization for reward-scale agnosticism, and 1% uniform mixing in categorical distributions. DreamerV3 became the **first algorithm to collect diamonds in Minecraft from scratch** — a long-standing AI challenge requiring long-horizon planning from pixels with sparse rewards in an open world. The Nature publication cemented world models as a serious paradigm for general RL.

**DreamerV4** (Hafner et al., September 2025) represents a major architectural shift, replacing the RSSM's GRU with a scalable block-causal transformer that jointly attends over spatial patches and temporal sequences. A novel "shortcut forcing" objective enables real-time interactive inference with only ~4 denoising steps. Critically, DreamerV4 can extract knowledge from diverse unlabeled videos with minimal action-labeled data, becoming the first agent to obtain diamonds in Minecraft purely from offline video-action data.

Running parallel to the Dreamer lineage, **MuZero** (Schrittwieser et al., DeepMind, Nature 2020) took a fundamentally different approach: learning a world model that never reconstructs observations. MuZero's three learned functions — representation (observation → hidden state), dynamics (state + action → reward + next state), and prediction (state → policy + value) — support Monte Carlo Tree Search planning. It **matched AlphaZero's superhuman performance on Go, chess, and shogi without knowing the rules**, while also setting new state-of-the-art on 57 Atari games. The implicit, non-generative representation makes MuZero computationally lean but unable to generate synthetic observations.

Other notable systems enriched the landscape. **SimPLe** (Kaiser et al., ICLR 2020) pioneered using video prediction models as world models for Atari, establishing the influential 100k benchmark. **IRIS** (Micheli et al., ICLR 2023) recast world modeling as sequence prediction by encoding frames into discrete VQ-VAE tokens and modeling dynamics with a GPT-like transformer, opening the door for language-model-style architectures in RL. **TD-MPC2** (Hansen et al., ICLR 2024) scaled implicit world models to 104 continuous control tasks with a single 317M-parameter multi-task agent. **DIAMOND** (Alonso et al., NeurIPS 2024 Spotlight) demonstrated that diffusion models operating in pixel space achieve superior world modeling — its mean human-normalized score of **1.46** on Atari 100k surpassed all prior methods, proving that "visual details matter."

The common architecture pattern across these systems follows a three-phase structure: encoder (observation → latent), latent dynamics model (predict next latent given action), and decoder or prediction heads. Two planning paradigms dominate: **background planning** (Dreamer, IRIS, DIAMOND), where actor-critic networks train on imagined rollouts for fast inference, and **decision-time planning** (MuZero, TD-MPC), where tree search or trajectory optimization runs at each step for greater precision. The field has progressed steadily toward generality, with DreamerV3 and TD-MPC2 both demonstrating fixed-hyperparameter operation across diverse domains, and DreamerV4 bridging toward foundation-model-scale video pretraining.

## The generative world model explosion of 2024–2026

Beginning in 2024, a fundamentally different framing emerged: massive generative models trained on internet video as implicit "world simulators." This represented a convergence of advances in video generation, diffusion models, and the foundation model paradigm.

**Sora** (OpenAI, February 2024) ignited the debate. OpenAI's technical report bore the provocative title "Video Generation Models as World Simulators," claiming that "scaling video generation models is a promising path towards building general purpose simulators of the physical world." Built on a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) architecture operating on spacetime patches, Sora generates up to one minute of high-fidelity video with emergent 3D consistency, object permanence, and cause-and-effect relationships. Sora 2 (September 2025) added audio generation, improved physics, and multi-shot controllability. Yet critics, including Gary Marcus, pointed to persistent physics violations — broken gravity, impossible collisions, violated object permanence — as evidence that Sora learns statistical pixel patterns rather than genuine physical understanding.

**Genie** (Google DeepMind, February 2024) pioneered a different approach: generative interactive environments. Trained on ~30,000 hours of 2D platformer gameplay with an 11B-parameter model, Genie learns a latent action space from unlabeled video and generates endless playable worlds from images, text, or sketches. **Genie 2** (December 2024) leapt to rich 3D interactive environments generated from a single image, demonstrating object interactions, NPC behavior, and counterfactual trajectories based on different actions. **Genie 3** (August 2025) — named a TIME Best Invention of 2025 — became the **first real-time interactive general-purpose world model**, generating navigable 3D worlds at 720p/24fps with consistency sustained for several minutes. It supports "promptable world events" that dynamically alter the simulation, and DeepMind positions it as "a key stepping stone on the path to AGI."

The neural game engine concept materialized with **GameNGen** (Google Research, August 2024), which runs classic DOOM entirely through a fine-tuned Stable Diffusion model at over 20 FPS, with human raters barely distinguishing real from simulated clips. **Oasis** (Decart/Etched, October 2024) demonstrated real-time Minecraft-like world generation at 20 FPS using a diffusion transformer, though with limited coherence and a "dream-like" quality. **GameGen-X** (HKUST/USTC, ICLR 2025) extended interactive generation to diverse open-world game genres using a Masked Spatial-Temporal Diffusion Transformer trained on over 4,000 hours of gameplay from 150+ games.

**NVIDIA Cosmos** (January 2025) positioned world models as infrastructure. Rather than a single model, Cosmos is a platform comprising multiple model families: Cosmos-Predict for future state prediction, Cosmos-Transfer for controllable world generation from structured inputs, Cosmos-Reason for chain-of-thought physical reasoning, and Cosmos-RL for reinforcement learning. Trained on **9,000 trillion tokens** from 20 million hours of video, Cosmos is open-weight and has been downloaded over 2 million times. Early adopters span autonomous driving (Uber, XPENG, Waabi), humanoid robotics (1X, Figure AI, Agility), and surgical robotics. Jensen Huang frames it as enabling "the ChatGPT moment for robotics."

**World Labs** (founded by Fei-Fei Li) launched **Marble** in November 2025 — the first commercial world model product, generating persistent, downloadable, editable 3D environments from text, images, or video. Unlike Genie's on-the-fly generation, Marble produces durable 3D assets exportable as Gaussian splats or meshes, with pricing from free to $95/month.

## LeCun's JEPA: the non-generative alternative

Yann LeCun has been the most vocal and influential critic of the generative world model paradigm, articulating an alternative vision in his 2022 position paper "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence." LeCun's proposed cognitive architecture comprises six modules — configurator, perception, world model, cost, memory, and actor — with the **Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA)** at its center.

JEPA differs fundamentally from generative approaches. Two encoder branches process inputs x and y into representations; a predictor module forecasts the representation of y from the representation of x, conditioned on a latent variable z that captures information about y not extractable from x. Predictions occur in **abstract representation space, not pixel space**. This means encoders can learn invariance to irrelevant details — textures, lighting fluctuations, leaves moving in wind — focusing only on semantically meaningful content.

LeCun's arguments against generative models are pointed. Predicting every pixel is computationally wasteful and fundamentally misguided because the physical world is non-deterministic at the pixel level. Autoregressive token prediction, whether in language or video, produces systems that discuss or depict physics without understanding it. LeCun has stated that LLMs "can't truly reason or plan, because they lack a model of the world" and predicted they will become "useless within five years."

The JEPA framework has been progressively implemented: **I-JEPA** (2023) for images, **V-JEPA** (February 2024) for video, and **V-JEPA 2** (June 2025), which scaled to 1.2B parameters trained on over 1 million hours of video. V-JEPA 2 achieved state-of-the-art action recognition and — critically — enabled **zero-shot robot planning** when its action-conditioned variant was trained on just 62 hours of unlabeled robot data. Meta claims V-JEPA 2 is 30× faster than Cosmos for comparable tasks.

In December 2025, LeCun left Meta to found **Advanced Machine Intelligence Labs (AMI Labs)** in Paris, closing a **$1.03 billion seed round** at a $3.5 billion valuation — Europe's largest seed round ever. Investors include NVIDIA, Toyota Ventures, Samsung, Jeff Bezos, and Eric Schmidt. AMI Labs represents the largest institutional bet in history on the thesis that world models, not LLMs, are the path to general intelligence.

A notable critique of the generative-vs-JEPA dichotomy appeared in a July 2025 paper ("Critiques of World Models") arguing that recursive application of JEPA's prediction function "is effectively autoregressive and generative" in latent space, suggesting the distinction may be less sharp than LeCun claims. The Schmidhuber-LeCun priority dispute adds another layer: Schmidhuber has publicly documented that his 1990–2015 work anticipated most elements of LeCun's 2022 proposal, including learning world models with multiple time scales, generating subgoals, and using intrinsic motivation.

## Physics-informed approaches: encoding nature's laws in neural architectures

At the opposite end of the spectrum from purely learned generative models lie approaches that embed physical laws directly into neural network architectures or training objectives. These provide strong inductive biases that improve generalization, data efficiency, and physical consistency.

**Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)**, introduced by Raissi, Perdikaris, and Karniadakis (2019, Brown/UPenn), encode PDEs directly into the loss function via automatic differentiation. The network approximates a solution u(x,t), and PDE residuals evaluated at collocation points serve as regularization alongside data-fitting terms. This mesh-free approach works for both forward problems (solving PDEs) and inverse problems (discovering unknown parameters). Variants include hp-VPINNs, cPINNs, and XPINNs for domain decomposition. Despite elegant formulation, PINNs face persistent challenges: spectral bias toward low-frequency solutions, complex loss landscapes with competing objectives, and accuracy that generally trails high-order numerical methods. Recent advances include PINNacle (NeurIPS 2024), a standardized benchmark across 15 canonical PDEs, and physics-informed distillation for automatic architecture discovery (Nature Communications, 2025).

**Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018 Best Paper, University of Toronto) replace discrete hidden layers with continuous dynamics dh/dt = f(h(t), t, θ), where f is a neural network. The adjoint sensitivity method enables O(1) memory training regardless of integration steps. **Neural SDEs** extend this to stochastic dynamics, better capturing inherent environmental uncertainty. Applications to world models include MoSim (2025), which combines rigid-body dynamics with Neural ODEs for long-horizon robot state prediction, achieving zero-shot RL by decoupling environment modeling from policy learning.

**Graph Neural Network simulators** provide perhaps the most natural neural architecture for particle-based physics. DeepMind's **Graph Network-based Simulator (GNS)** (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., ICML 2020) represents physical states as particles (graph nodes) and computes dynamics via learned message-passing on edges. Training noise corruption prevents error accumulation over long rollouts. **MeshGraphNets** (Pfaff et al., ICLR 2021) extended this to mesh-based Eulerian simulations for aerodynamics, structural mechanics, and cloth. Recent advances include diffusion graph networks for complex fluid simulations (ICLR 2025) and curvature-aware attention incorporating Riemann curvature tensors for PDEs on manifolds (ICML 2025).

**Hamiltonian Neural Networks** (Greydanus et al., NeurIPS 2019) parameterize the Hamiltonian H(q,p) with a neural network and derive dynamics via Hamilton's equations, learning exact energy conservation in an unsupervised manner. **Lagrangian Neural Networks** (Cranmer et al., Princeton/DeepMind, 2020) offer the same conservation guarantees without requiring canonical coordinates, working with arbitrary generalized coordinates. Extensions include constrained variants (CHNN/CLNN, NeurIPS 2020) that achieve lower error with 20 training trajectories than standard Neural ODEs with 1,600, and momentum-conserving versions that preserve translational and rotational symmetries.

**Differentiable physics simulators** make end-to-end backpropagation through physical simulation possible. **DiffTaichi** (Hu et al., MIT, ICLR 2020) provides a domain-specific differentiable programming language that is **188× faster than TensorFlow** for physics simulation while generating exact gradients. **Brax** (Google DeepMind, 2021), written in JAX, achieves millions of physics steps per second on TPUs and integrates directly with RL training. These enable gradient-based optimization through physics for system identification, trajectory optimization, and sim-to-real transfer.

**Neural operators** represent a paradigm shift from solving individual PDE instances to learning mappings between function spaces. The **Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)** (Li et al., Caltech, ICLR 2021) parameterizes integral kernels in Fourier space, achieving up to **three orders of magnitude speedup** over traditional PDE solvers with resolution-invariant operation. **DeepONet** (Lu et al., Brown, 2019) uses a branch-trunk architecture that works with complex geometries and sparse data. These operators serve as physics surrogates that, once trained, can evaluate new PDE configurations in milliseconds rather than hours.

The frontier is moving toward physics foundation models that generalize across physical systems. **Universal Physics Transformers (UPTs)** (Alkin et al., JKU Linz, 2024) provide a unified architecture for both Lagrangian and Eulerian simulations handling up to 4.2 million input points. **General Physics Transformer (GPhyT)** (Wiesner et al., September 2025) trains a hybrid transformer-neural-differentiator with classical numerical integrator on **1.8 TB of diverse simulation data**, achieving 7× better performance than specialized architectures with zero-shot generalization to unseen physical systems via in-context learning.

**PhysGaussian** (Xie et al., UCLA/Utah, CVPR 2024 Highlight) unifies simulation and rendering by integrating continuum mechanics with 3D Gaussian Splatting. Each Gaussian kernel carries both visual attributes and physical properties (deformation gradient, stress tensor), evolving via the Material Point Method. This "what you see is what you simulate" paradigm directly connects visual world models with physically grounded dynamics.

## Hybrid architectures that blend learning with physics

The most promising direction may be hybrid approaches that combine the generalization of neural networks with the guarantees of physical laws. These span a spectrum of integration strategies.

**Soft constraints** (learning bias) add physics-based penalty terms to loss functions. Physics-Informed Neural Operators (PINO) combine operator learning with physics-informed losses, often requiring **5–10× less data** than pure data-driven approaches. **Hard constraints** (inductive bias) build physics into network architecture: Hamiltonian and Lagrangian networks enforce conservation laws by construction, while equivariant networks respect spatial symmetries. **Hybrid neural-physics** models use differentiable physics engines for known dynamics and neural networks for unknown residuals. NeuralSim (Heiden et al., ICRA 2021) augments differentiable simulators with neural networks to learn residual dynamics that close the sim-to-real gap.

GPhyT exemplifies the emerging pattern: its transformer serves as a neural differentiator while an explicit numerical integrator enforces temporal integration structure. This separation of concerns — neural networks for spatial differentiation, classical methods for time stepping — produces stable long-horizon rollouts that neither component achieves alone. A 2024 MIT thesis on "Building World Models with Neural Physics" demonstrated modular hybrid frameworks where basic dynamics come from differentiable physics and additional modules are learned, enabling plug-and-play generalization to novel configurations.

The convergence trajectory is clear: from specialized single-physics models toward universal, multi-physics foundation models that embed physical structure while retaining the flexibility to learn unknown dynamics from data.

## World models enter the physical world: robotics and autonomous driving

The translation of world models from game environments to physical robots represents one of the field's most active frontiers.

**DayDreamer** (Hafner et al., NeurIPS 2022) applied DreamerV2 to physical robots directly in the real world, training a quadruped to walk from scratch in **one hour** and adapting to perturbations in ten minutes. Robotic arms learned pick-and-place from camera images approaching human teleoperation performance. This established that world model-based RL can work online in reality, not just simulation.

**RoboDreamer** (Zhou et al., MIT/HKUST, ICML 2024) factorizes video generation into compositional diffusion models conditioned on parsed language primitives, enabling compositional generalization to new instructions. **DreMa** (ICLR 2025) reconceives robot world models as learnable digital twins using Gaussian Splatting integrated with physics simulators, bridging explicit 3D representations with learned dynamics. **V-JEPA 2's action-conditioned variant** enables zero-shot robot planning on unseen environments after training on just 62 hours of unlabeled robot data.

Autonomous driving has become the most mature application domain. **GAIA-1** (Wayve, September 2023) uses a 9B-parameter transformer to generate realistic driving scenarios with fine-grained control, demonstrating emergent geometry understanding. **DriveDreamer** (ECCV 2024) builds driving world models from real scenarios using diffusion models. Multiple 2025 surveys catalog dozens of systems spanning video generation, 4D occupancy prediction, and LiDAR simulation for driving. **WorldBench** (UCLA, January 2026) provides concept-specific evaluation of physics reasoning in driving scenarios, finding that all current state-of-the-art models achieve only **~45% mIoU** on physics benchmarks — far from reliable deployment.

**NVIDIA Cosmos** has become the dominant infrastructure platform for robotics world models. Its adoption by 1X (humanoid robots), Figure AI, Agility Robotics, Uber, XPENG, and Waabi demonstrates commercial traction. **GEN-0** (Generalist AI, November 2025) claims to establish scaling laws for robotics by training on "millions of diverse activities" across homes, factories, and warehouses.

Key challenges persist. Sim-to-real transfer remains fragile. Real-time planning with transformer or diffusion models conflicts with control-frequency requirements. Physical fidelity is insufficient for safety-critical deployment. And long-horizon prediction degrades over extended rollouts — a critical limitation for complex manipulation tasks.

## The definitional question: what counts as a world model?

Perhaps the deepest tension in the field is definitional. The term "world model" now means fundamentally different things to different communities. In cognitive science, it denotes Craik's internal mental representation used for prediction and reasoning. In model-based RL, it means a learned transition function. In the generative AI community, it has become nearly synonymous with video generation. For LeCun, it refers specifically to a predictive module operating in abstract representation space.

A 2024 ACM Computing Surveys paper noted the definition remains "a subject of ongoing debate, generally divided into two primary perspectives: understanding the world and predicting the future." A July 2025 analysis argued that "a world model is not fundamentally about generating videos, but about serving as a sandbox for reasoning and thought-experiment."

The most significant empirical contribution to this debate came from Google DeepMind in 2025, which published a formal result proving that **any agent capable of generalizing to a broad range of simple goal-directed tasks must have learned a predictive model capable of simulating its environment** — and this model can always be recovered from the agent. This suggests world models are not merely useful but mathematically necessary for general intelligence.

An ICLR 2025 paper examining whether video generation models learn physical laws found that diffusion-based models trained on 2D physics simulations achieve perfect in-distribution generalization and measurable combinatorial generalization, but **fail on out-of-distribution scenarios**, exhibiting "case-based" generalization rather than abstracting general physical rules. The authors conclude that "scaling alone is insufficient for video generation models to uncover fundamental physical laws."

The emerging consensus distinguishes between several categories: pixel-space generative models (Sora, Genie, GameNGen) that produce visually plausible simulations, latent-space predictive models (V-JEPA) that operate on abstract representations, 3D spatial models (Marble) that generate persistent environments, RL dynamics models (Dreamer, MuZero) that support planning, and physics-informed models (PINNs, HNNs, GNS) that encode natural laws. Whether these are all "world models" or fundamentally different things remains genuinely unresolved.

## Conclusion: convergence, open questions, and the stakes ahead

The world model landscape in early 2026 is defined by three converging trends and three unresolved tensions.

**The convergence.** First, scale: DreamerV4, Cosmos, and V-JEPA 2 all demonstrate that world models benefit from foundation-model-scale pretraining on diverse video data, mirroring the scaling revolution in language models. Second, generality: DreamerV3's fixed-hyperparameter operation across 150+ tasks and GPhyT's zero-shot generalization to unseen physics domains show that universal world models are feasible. Third, commercialization: Marble, Cosmos, and the billion-dollar capitalizations of AMI Labs and World Labs signal that world models have crossed from research curiosity to industrial infrastructure.

**The tensions.** The fundamental architectural question — whether to predict pixels (generative), abstract representations (JEPA), or physics-structured states (PINNs/HNNs) — remains unresolved, with major labs making divergent bets. The fidelity gap between what world models can simulate and what safety-critical applications require is enormous; WorldBench's 45% mIoU finding suggests deployment-grade physical fidelity is years away. And the deepest question — whether any amount of scaling will produce world models that genuinely understand causality rather than merely correlating patterns — may require not just better models but new theoretical frameworks to answer.

What is no longer in dispute is the centrality of the idea itself. From Craik's 1943 intuition through Schmidhuber's 1990 RNN dreams to the current moment, the proposition that intelligence requires building and using internal models of the world has only grown stronger. DeepMind's formal proof that general agents must contain world models, combined with DreamerV3's empirical success and V-JEPA 2's zero-shot robot planning, suggest this is not merely a useful paradigm but a fundamental principle. The next years will determine whether the principle can be realized at the scale and fidelity the world demands.
